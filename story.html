Our Story

From a village in
Kilimanjaro
to a continent's data.

A boy who witnessed the cost of human-wildlife conflict grew up to build the digital infrastructure that helps prevent it. This is the story of WDA-Lab.

I knew that blaming either the farmers or the animals wouldn't solve the problem. The true missing link was awareness — and I wanted to be part of the solution.

01

The Beginning

Conflict in the shadow of Kilimanjaro

Growing up in the Kilimanjaro region of Tanzania, the reality of human-wildlife conflict was not an abstract concept — it was part of daily life. Julius watched animals, driven by resource scarcity and habitat encroachment, raid local crops. In response, villagers fought back to protect their livelihoods, often resulting in the tragic killing of wildlife.

Watching this cycle unfold, he felt a deep sense of sorrow — but also a profound calling. The animals were not villains. The farmers were not criminals. They were both victims of a system where information was scarce, decisions were reactive, and the consequences were irreversible.

"I realized that the true missing link was awareness. And I knew, even then, that I wanted to be part of the solution."

That conviction — formed in a Kilimanjaro village — would go on to shape everything that followed.

02

The Foundation

Science, resilience, and an unexpected detour

To understand the root of the problem, Julius pursued a degree in Wildlife Management at the College of African Wildlife Management, Mweka — one of Africa's most respected institutions. Studying animal ecology, behavior transformed his perspective. He came to understand that human encroachment forces these interactions, and that community awareness backed by hard data is conservation's strongest shield.

But the path to a conservation career is rarely a straight line. After graduation, Julius faced the harsh reality of unemployment. To survive, he took a role as a hardware store clerk — a world away from the savannah.

"What seemed like a detour became a gift. I mastered inventory systems, database management, and structured data control — skills that would later become my secret weapon."

The discipline of managing physical stock translated directly into the discipline of managing biological data. Without knowing it, Julius was building the foundation of what WDA-Lab would become.

03

The Turning Point

Mentorship, technology, and a new purpose

The true breakthrough came when Julius joined the Centre for Research Cooperation (CRC) in Tanzania. Under the mentorship and guidance of Howard Frederick, his background in wildlife biology collided with his passion for systems management in a way that changed everything.

He immersed himself in the world of wildlife data annotation, entry, management, and analysis. He saw firsthand how labeling aerial sensor image, camera trap images, analyzing output of the data, mapping and managing migration datasets could predict animal movements — and prevent conflict before it even started.

"I saw how the right data, in the right hands, at the right time, could prevent the very scenes I witnessed growing up. That was the moment everything became clear."

04

The Launch

When the contract ended, the mission began

When Julius's contract at CRC ended in 2023, he did not see a closing door. He saw an opening. Armed with field expertise, data skills, and an intimate understanding of what conservation organizations truly needed, he launched Wild Data Annotation Lab.

The premise was simple but powerful: conservation organizations & Researchers across Africa were sitting on enormous volumes of raw wildlife data — camera trap images, field records, migration logs — but lacked the specialized capacity to process, structure, and analyze it at the speed and scale modern conservation demands.

WDA Lab would be the bridge. Not a generic data company, but a team of field-trained specialists who understood both the science and the stakes — people who could look at an annotated image and understand what it meant for the ecosystem it came from.

"We do not just process wildlife data. We understand it."
